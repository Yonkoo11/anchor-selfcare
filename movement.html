Movement & Posture

Basic body mechanics explained without jargon. Your body already knows how to do most of this. These instructions just remind it.

General wellness information. This is not medical advice. If you have health concerns, please consult a healthcare provider.

If you work at a screen

Sitting loads your spine 40% more than standing. Slouching makes it worse. Your eyes weren't designed for close-up focus for hours. Here's the minimum:

-
20-20-20 Rule: Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Lets your eye muscles relax.
-
Movement Snacks: Stand up once an hour. Roll your shoulders. Walk to get water. Small movements add up.
-
Blink More: You blink 66% less when staring at screens. Dry eyes = tired eyes. Blink deliberately.

Good posture is not rigid, military, or forced. It's stacked. Head over shoulders, shoulders over hips.

  1. 1Imagine a string attached to the top of your head.
  2. 2Imagine that string gently pulling you upward.
  3. 3Let everything else hang from that point.
  4. 4Your shoulders drop naturally. Your chest opens slightly.
  5. 5That's it. That's good posture.

You don't have to hold this perfectly. Just reset when you notice you've collapsed.

Why it matters: When you slouch, your spine handles both compression and shear forces. Sitting upright with a natural curve keeps pressure distributed evenly across your discs.

A quick way to find neutral posture when you've been slouching. Takes ten seconds.

  1. 1Stand with your back against a wall.
  2. 2Touch the wall with your heels, butt, shoulders, and head.
  3. 3This might feel exaggerated. That's okay.
  4. 4Step away from the wall.
  5. 5Try to keep roughly that alignment.

Do this whenever you notice you've been hunched over.

Balance keeps you stable and confident. It's simple to practice. You can do this while waiting for anything.

  1. 1Stand near a wall or counter, in case you need support.
  2. 2Lift one foot slightly off the ground.
  3. 3Hold for ten seconds. It's okay to wobble.
  4. 4Switch feet.
  5. 5That's it.

When this gets easy, try it with your eyes closed.

A push-up is just pushing your body away from something. Start wherever you need to start.

  1. 1Start easy: hands on a wall, push away and back.
  2. 2Next level: hands on a counter or sturdy table.
  3. 3Next: hands on the floor, knees down.
  4. 4Finally: full push-up, only toes and hands touching ground.
  5. 5Body stays straight like a plank. Lower until chest almost touches. Push back up.

One push-up is better than zero. Start where you are.

Most back pain comes from bending wrong. You should bend from your hips, not your spine.

  1. 1Stand with feet hip-width apart.
  2. 2Push your butt back, like you're closing a car door with it.
  3. 3Your knees will bend slightly. That's fine.
  4. 4Your back stays flat, not rounded.
  5. 5This is how you pick things up without hurting yourself.

Practice this with nothing in your hands first. Then use it every time you lift.

Why it matters: Bending with a rounded spine puts shear force on your discs - they're being compressed and pushed forward at the same time. Hinging at the hips keeps the load vertical and distributed.

If you've been staring at a screen, your neck is probably jutting forward and your shoulders are hunched. This takes 30 seconds to fix.

  1. 1Drop your shoulders down and back. Let them fall.
  2. 2Tuck your chin slightly - like you're making a double chin.
  3. 3Hold for 5 seconds. Release.
  4. 4Roll your shoulders backward 5 times.
  5. 5Tilt your head to one side (ear toward shoulder). Hold 10 seconds. Switch.

Do this every time you notice tension building. Prevention beats pain.
